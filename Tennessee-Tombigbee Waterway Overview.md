# Tennessee-Tombigbee Waterway (Tenn-Tom) — Overview

## What it is
A 234-mile mostly artificial waterway connecting the **Tennessee River** (at Pickwick Lake, near the Mississippi/Alabama/Tennessee corner) to the **Tombigbee River**, where it meets the Black Warrior River near **Demopolis, AL** — the exact spot you were standing at today.

It gives Midwest/Tennessee Valley river traffic a direct shortcut to the **Gulf of Mexico via Mobile**, instead of the old, much longer route down the Mississippi.

## Why it exists
- The Tennessee River flows east–west across northern Alabama and swings west into Mississippi, coming within roughly 500 miles of the Gulf — before turning north toward the Ohio River instead.
- Without the Tenn-Tom, cargo from that region had to travel hundreds of extra miles out of the way to reach the Gulf.
- The idea dates back to the early 1800s. A federal feasibility study happened in **1874** under Grant — engineering said yes, economics said not yet.
- Real political momentum didn't arrive until **1946** (Sen. John Stennis & Rep. Jamie Whitten of Mississippi — both now have locks named after them).

## Building it
- Construction: **1972–1984** (12 years), funded ~**$2 billion**
- Involved removing roughly **310 million cubic yards of earth** — more than was moved building the Panama Canal
- Centerpiece engineering feat: the **Divide Cut**, a 27-mile canal cutting up to 175 feet deep through the land dividing the Tennessee and Tombigbee watersheds
- Completed **Dec 12, 1984**; opened to commercial traffic **Jan 14, 1985**; formally dedicated **June 1, 1985** in Columbus, MS and Mobile, AL
- Went through NEPA environmental review (one of the first big water projects to do so) — a ~50-mile levee was added specifically to protect wildlife habitat that would've flooded otherwise

## The system itself
- **10 locks and dams**, total lift of 341 feet
- Locks: 110 ft wide × 600 ft long, handle tows up to 8 barges at once
- Operate **24/7**
- Channel depth: 9 ft (river sections), 12 ft (canal/divide sections)

## Where it fits with what you saw today
Demopolis Lock — the one at the barge/train crossing — is the **first lock below the confluence** of the Black Warrior and Tombigbee, meaning literally all Tenn-Tom traffic heading to Mobile funnels through it. That's why it's the busiest lock on the whole 10-lock system, and why you caught two barges in one morning without even trying.

## Loose threads to explore later
- Yellow Creek Inland Port (Mississippi) — steel and forestry industry tied to the northern end of the waterway
- Whitten Lock — third-highest single-lift lock east of the Rockies
- Political history: Kennedy reportedly intended to back it before his assassination; Nixon later pushed initial funding as part of a "Southern Strategy" visit to Mobile in 1971
